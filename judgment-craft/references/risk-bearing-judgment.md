# Risk-Bearing Judgment

Use this discipline when a judgment becomes a bet: capital, reputation, safety, authority, an irreversible commitment, or consequences borne by other people. The aim is not maximum boldness or maximum expected value. The aim is a real edge expressed at a size that preserves survival, learning, and legitimate choice.

## Core Distinction

```text
Sound risk = calibrated edge × survivable sizing × reversible learning × updating discipline
Bad risk   = overconfidence × leverage × one model × externalized downside
```

Treat this as a checklist, not a literal equation. A positive expected value is insufficient when one failure ends future participation, violates a right or hard constraint, or transfers losses to people who did not choose the bet.

## 1. Classify The Decision World

- **Small world:** rules are stable, trials repeat, feedback is frequent, outcomes closely test the model, and the decision-maker bears most consequences. Probability, game theory, and expected value can be optimized aggressively inside this boundary.
- **Large world:** rules or participants adapt, evidence is sparse, the event may be one-off, values resist one metric, power changes incentives, or errors affect non-participants. Use models as inputs, not sovereign decision-makers.
- **Mixed world:** isolate the repeatable/model-friendly part, but govern sizing, irreversibility, rights, and externalities with large-world constraints.

When uncertain, classify upward. Do not import confidence earned in a small world into a large one without new evidence.

## 2. Separate Edge From Exposure

For the crux, record:

- the plausible outcome range, not one forecast
- the evidence and base rate behind the probabilities or confidence bands
- upside, downside, and who receives each
- maximum tolerable loss and whether another attempt remains possible
- leverage, correlation, hidden tail risk, and ways the model can be jointly wrong

Size the smallest bet that can resolve the decisive uncertainty. Increase exposure only after the predeclared evidence arrives. Never let conviction substitute for a survival bound.

## 3. Preserve Option Value

Prefer staged, reversible commitments when information is incomplete:

- pilot before rollout
- observation position before full position
- feature flag before irreversible migration
- option to delay, exit, expand, abandon, or switch

Optionality is not permanent indecision. Name the information being purchased, its deadline, and the condition that forces commit, exit, or escalation.

## 4. Choose Robust Baseline Before Exploitation

Start from a baseline that is difficult to punish when evidence is weak. Depart from it only when a counterparty, market, or system shows a stable and repeatable bias.

Every targeted deviation creates a counter-exposure: the other side may adapt, the pattern may decay, or the intervention may reveal your own weakness. Record that new vulnerability and the signal that ends the deviation.

## 5. Price Edge Decay And Friction

An apparent advantage is not usable edge until it survives:

- competition and adaptation
- fees, latency, maintenance, and coordination cost
- capacity, liquidity, access, permission, and relationship constraints
- the time between discovery and execution

State the edge's expected half-life and refresh condition. Being right after the opportunity closes is not an advantage.

## 6. Calibrate Intuition

Trust intuition only when it was trained on relevant, repeated decisions with timely and legible feedback. In sparse, adaptive, or one-shot domains, intuition is a hypothesis—record it, test it, and lower confidence. Bodily discomfort can flag an anomaly; it cannot explain one.

## 7. Add Large-World Safeguards

When a decision affects non-participants or creates durable power, apply three hard constraints:

- **Autonomy:** affected people have informed, real, executable choice where possible.
- **Plurality:** preserve independent challenge, competing models, fallback paths, and limits on concentrated authority.
- **Reciprocity:** use only rules you would accept if decision-maker and downside-bearer exchanged places.

Do not convert these constraints into expected-value terms that a sufficiently large claimed benefit can erase. Escalate decisions that are irreversible, civilization-scale, rights-affecting, or materially externalize downside.

## 8. Review Process Separately From Outcome

Before the result, timestamp the information set, crux, confidence range, sizing logic, model limits, falsifier, and update rule. After the result, grade two things separately:

1. **Process quality:** Was the decision reasonable from the evidence available then?
2. **Outcome quality:** What happened, how much was skill versus variance, and what new evidence changes the model?

A good decision can lose and a bad decision can win. Never let a lucky outcome validate an unfalsifiable process or an unlucky outcome erase a calibrated one.

```text
World: small / large / mixed — why?
Range and confidence:
Edge and expected half-life:
Maximum loss / survival bound:
Reversible first step and option purchased:

```
