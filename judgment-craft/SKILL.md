# Judgment Craft (judgment in the AI era)

## Meta-core: the one thing that can't be outsourced

In the AI era, **computation and knowledge can be outsourced; judgment cannot.** Judgment's essence is the cognitive ability to **grasp the few key variables inside complexity** — to choose the right *direction*.

> **"Even the best AI can only compute for you; only you can decide the direction of the computation."**

That one sentence answers three questions at once: *what insight is* (picking the right direction = the right variables), *why judgment is irreplaceable* (the direction is set by a human), and *why you must practice* (a sense of direction can only be forged, never read off a page). When knowledge is at your fingertips and computation is nearly free, the scarce moat is **judgment forged through lived practice**.

The three pillars below form one chain; together they are the operating system that produces and protects judgment.

---

## Pillar 1 — Insight = signal vs noise, not enumerating variables (the crux)

### The three stages of cognition

1. **Shallow simplicity (此岸的简单)** — "good idea + effort = success." Few variables, high confidence, naive. You haven't yet seen what you're missing.
2. **Complexity** — the variables explode; everything "depends"; confusion and paralysis. Necessary to pass through, but **not the destination** — a memo that just enumerates twenty factors is stuck here.
3. **Earned / profound simplicity (彼岸的简单)** — you distill the **Pareto core**: you know which **one or two variables actually drive the result**, and treat the rest as secondary or context.

> Oliver Wendell Holmes: *"I would not give a fig for the simplicity on this side of complexity, but I would give my life for the simplicity on the other side of complexity."*

### Why this is the essence

It overturns the illusion that **considering more factors makes you more sophisticated**. Naval's one-line calls ("it's a good deal because X") look careless to a beginner; in fact he knows *which X is decisive*. **Focusing on the few right factors beats exhaustively listing all factors.** The skill is not having more variables — it's knowing which ones to ignore.

### The trap: false (this-side) simplicity

A one-liner that **skipped** the complexity is not insight — it's stage 1 wearing stage 3's clothes. Earned simplicity must **survive the complex view**: you went through all the variables, *then* collapsed them. Test yourself — can you defend why the factors you dropped don't matter? If not, you're on the wrong side of complexity.

### How to find the crux

1. **Map the variable space first** — go through the complexity, don't skip it.
2. **Rank by impact × uncertainty.** The crux is **high-impact AND genuinely in doubt**. An important-but-settled factor is not the crux; neither is an uncertain factor that barely moves the result.
3. **Name the crux explicitly:** "This thesis lives or dies on **X** (and maybe Y)." One or two, rarely three.
4. **Pressure-test it:** if X resolves one way, does the conclusion follow *almost regardless of the other factors*? If yes, X is the real driver. If the answer still hinges on five other things, you haven't found it yet.
5. **Deliberately demote the rest** — say which factors you are consciously treating as secondary, and why they don't swing the answer.

### Insight training

**Repeatedly ask "what are the one or two variables that truly drive the result here?"** — then check, over time, whether you picked right. Examples of a crux (not the full dashboard): Cloudflare → large-customer net adds / DBNRR / gross margin; a lossy option-like name → the single milestone + cash runway; a "cheap but breaking" value name → estimate-revision direction, not the headline multiple; a capex-heavy AI investor → whether that spend earns ROIC > WACC.

## Pillar 2 — Cognitive clarity sets decision cost; judgment is forged, not outsourced

- **"If a decision is agonizing and effortful, the real problem is that your understanding isn't yet clear enough."** Once you see the underlying logic, the decision becomes effortless — an obvious necessity. Don't push harder on the decision; push harder on the clarity.
- **Intuition is the brain's compression of massive information into a shortcut** — judgment forged into intuition. It feels instant because the work was done earlier.
- **You can't outsource taste / a feeling / an experience.** AI can take over knowledge and computation, but the feeling, the lived experience, and the judgment and taste forged from them cannot be outsourced — unless you actually do the thing yourself. **Judgment is trained, not read.**

---

## Pillar 3 — Fight vagueness & wishful thinking (the operating system)

- **Anxiety and churn are, at root, vagueness.** Write the trouble as structure — objective function / resource constraints / option payoffs. *"Defining the problem is half of solving it."* Much repetitive "thinking" is elegant procrastination.
- **"A falsifiable wrong prediction is 100× more valuable than an unverifiable correct intuition."** The wrong-but-falsifiable one sends you back to check *which assumption broke* — a feedback loop. The vague-but-right one teaches nothing.
- **Independent thinking is grounded in evidence and logic**, not credulity — don't blindly trust others' words, your own eyes, sell-side research, or the algorithmic feed — and **hedge wishful thinking with the strongest opposing view.**

---

## How the three chain together

> Grasp the key variables (signal vs noise) → form a clear cognition → clarity makes the decision effortless (judgment = compressed intuition) → but that judgment can only be forged through lived practice + structuring + falsification; it cannot be outsourced to AI.

Pillar 1 finds the right variables; Pillar 2 is what clear cognition *feels like* and why it must be earned; Pillar 3 is the discipline that actually produces clarity (it doesn't arrive by itself, and it is the most counter-human — people love vagueness because it postpones the decision, and love wishful thinking).

## How to use it

- **Lead with the crux** (Pillar 1): name the 1-2 decisive variables; let everything else be supporting context, not a flat list of equally-weighted metrics.
- **Don't outsource the thinking to AI.** AI summaries can filter and accelerate, but the cognition itself must be built by reading the primary source yourself and **betting to validate**. Use AI for breadth; build judgment by depth + skin in the game.
- **Before you bet, write the falsification trigger:** "what data, if it appears, makes me admit I'm wrong?" — *then* commit. Expose the judgment to being disproven; that is the only way it improves.
- **When a decision churns, suspect vagueness:** structure it (goal / constraints / payoffs) before deciding, instead of looping.
- **Hedge wishful thinking** by writing the strongest bear/opposing case before concluding.

## Relationship to other skills

- `alpha-research` depends on this skill: the key-driver step and the five-lens synthesis must **lead with the crux** (Pillar 1), and the staged-decision **falsification trigger is written before committing** (Pillar 3).
- Pillar 3 is the *soul* of a buy-side memo's **"thesis breakpoint / falsification trigger."**
- Complements **`research-craft`** (how to run good research): judgment-craft is *how to think and decide*; research-craft is *how to investigate*.
